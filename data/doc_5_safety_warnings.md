# Safety: Warning Signs and When to Get Help

This page explains general warning signs for people living with heart failure
and related conditions. It is educational only. If you are ever unsure, it is
always safer to seek medical help.

## Call emergency services right away

Some symptoms can signal a medical emergency and need immediate help. Call your
local emergency number (911 in Canada and the US) or go to the nearest emergency
department if you have chest pain, pressure, or tightness; severe or sudden
shortness of breath; fainting or passing out; severe dizziness; a fast or
irregular heartbeat with feeling faint; coughing up blood; or sudden weakness,
numbness, trouble speaking, or face drooping, which can be signs of a stroke.
Do not wait to see if these symptoms pass.

## Contact your care team soon (same day or next day)

Some changes are not emergencies but should be reported promptly. These include
a weight gain of more than about 2 kilograms (4 to 5 pounds) over two to three
days; more swelling in the legs, ankles, or abdomen; needing more pillows than
usual to breathe at night; more shortness of breath with your usual activities;
a persistent cough or wheeze; or new or worsening tiredness. Reporting these
early can prevent a hospital stay.

## Be careful with medicines

Do not start or stop heart or blood pressure medicines without talking to your
care team. Over-the-counter anti-inflammatory painkillers (such as ibuprofen and
similar drugs) can cause the body to hold on to fluid and may not be safe for
some people with heart failure, so check before using them. Always tell any new
clinician or pharmacist that you have heart failure.

## General safety reminders

Keep an up-to-date medicine list with you. Know your "normal" so you can spot
changes early. Make sure someone close to you knows your conditions and what to
do in an emergency. If a symptom frightens you or feels different and serious,
treat it as urgent and seek help rather than waiting.

## This tool's limits

An information tool like this cannot examine you, cannot see your test results,
and cannot handle emergencies. It is not a substitute for your doctor, a nurse
line, or emergency services. For anything urgent, contact real medical help.

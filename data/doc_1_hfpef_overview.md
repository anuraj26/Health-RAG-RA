# Understanding HFpEF (Heart Failure with Preserved Ejection Fraction)

## What HFpEF is

Heart failure with preserved ejection fraction, often shortened to HFpEF, is a
type of heart failure in which the heart's main pumping chamber (the left
ventricle) squeezes out a normal amount of blood with each beat, but the
chamber has become stiff and does not relax and fill properly between beats.
"Ejection fraction" is the percentage of blood pumped out of the left ventricle
with each heartbeat. In HFpEF the ejection fraction is normal or near normal,
usually 50 percent or higher, which is why it is called "preserved."

Because the heart muscle is stiff, pressure can build up inside the heart and
back up into the lungs and the rest of the body. This is what causes many of
the symptoms people feel.

## Common symptoms

People with HFpEF often notice shortness of breath, especially during activity
or when lying flat, tiredness and low energy, and swelling in the legs, ankles,
or abdomen from fluid building up. Some people wake up at night feeling short of
breath, or find they need extra pillows to sleep comfortably. Rapid weight gain
over a few days can be a sign that the body is holding on to extra fluid.

## Who tends to get HFpEF

HFpEF becomes more common with age and is more frequently diagnosed in women. It
is strongly linked to other long-term conditions, especially high blood
pressure, diabetes, obesity, atrial fibrillation, and chronic kidney disease.
Many people with HFpEF have several of these conditions at the same time, and
controlling them is a central part of care.

## How it is diagnosed

Diagnosis usually involves a review of symptoms, a physical examination, blood
tests (including markers such as natriuretic peptides), and an echocardiogram, an
ultrasound of the heart that measures the ejection fraction and shows how well
the heart relaxes and fills. Other tests may be used to rule out different
causes of the symptoms.

## Why it matters

HFpEF is a long-term condition, but many people live active lives with it when
it is well managed. The goals of care are to relieve symptoms, reduce fluid
buildup, treat the underlying conditions that drive it, and lower the chance of
being admitted to hospital. Working closely with a care team and keeping up with
follow-up visits makes a real difference.

# Treatment Options for HFpEF

This is general education about the kinds of treatments doctors consider for
heart failure with preserved ejection fraction (HFpEF). It is not a treatment
plan. Decisions about medicines always belong with your own doctor, who knows
your full history.

## The two big goals

Treatment for HFpEF generally aims to do two things: relieve symptoms (mostly by
removing extra fluid) and treat the underlying conditions that make HFpEF worse,
such as high blood pressure, diabetes, obesity, atrial fibrillation, and kidney
disease.

## Medicines that may be used

SGLT2 inhibitors are a group of medicines (examples include empagliflozin and
dapagliflozin) that were first used for diabetes but are now recommended in
guidelines for many people with HFpEF, because studies have shown they can lower
the risk of being hospitalised for heart failure. They are used in people with
and without diabetes.

Diuretics, sometimes called water pills, help the body get rid of extra fluid.
They are used to ease symptoms like breathlessness and swelling. They treat the
congestion but not the underlying stiffness of the heart.

Mineralocorticoid receptor antagonists, such as spironolactone, may be
considered in some people with HFpEF and can affect potassium levels, so blood
tests are used to monitor them.

Medicines for blood pressure are important, because keeping blood pressure
controlled reduces strain on the heart. The specific choice depends on the
person and any other conditions they have.

## Treating the conditions behind HFpEF

Because HFpEF is so closely tied to other conditions, a large part of treatment
is managing those: controlling blood sugar in diabetes, supporting healthy
weight loss in people with obesity, managing atrial fibrillation, and protecting
kidney function. Improvements in these areas often improve heart failure
symptoms too.

## Lifestyle and self-care

Alongside medicines, doctors usually recommend limiting salt, monitoring fluid
and daily weight, staying physically active within safe limits, and attending
regular follow-up. Cardiac rehabilitation programs can help some people build
activity safely.

## Questions worth asking your doctor

Helpful questions include: which of my conditions is driving my heart failure
the most; is an SGLT2 inhibitor right for me; how should I track my weight and
fluid; what symptoms mean I should call you versus go to emergency; and how
often should my kidney function and potassium be checked.

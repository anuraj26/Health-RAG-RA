# Living Well with Heart Failure: Daily Self-Management

This is general patient-education information about day-to-day self-care for
people living with heart failure, including HFpEF. Your own care team may give
you different, personalised targets — always follow their guidance for your
situation.

## Watch your weight

Sudden weight gain can be one of the earliest signs that the body is holding on
to extra fluid. Many people are asked to weigh themselves at the same time each
morning, after using the bathroom and before eating, and to write it down. A gain
of more than about 2 kilograms (around 4 to 5 pounds) over two to three days is
often a signal to contact the care team, because it may mean fluid is building up.

## Be mindful of salt and fluids

Salt (sodium) makes the body hold on to water, which can worsen swelling and
breathlessness. Many people with heart failure are advised to limit added salt
and salty processed foods. Some are also given a daily fluid limit. The exact
numbers vary from person to person, so these targets should come from your care
team rather than a general rule.

## Take medicines as prescribed

Taking heart failure medicines consistently, at the right times, helps keep
symptoms stable. If a medicine causes side effects, it is better to call the care
team than to stop on your own. Keep an up-to-date list of all medicines to bring
to appointments.

## Stay active within safe limits

Gentle, regular activity such as walking is usually encouraged and can improve
energy and quality of life. Activity should be built up slowly and paused if it
causes chest discomfort, severe breathlessness, or dizziness. A cardiac
rehabilitation program can help people exercise safely.

## Track your symptoms

It helps to notice and write down changes such as more shortness of breath, more
swelling, needing more pillows to sleep, waking up breathless, or new tiredness.
Bringing these notes to appointments helps the care team adjust treatment early.

## Keep up with appointments and tests

Regular follow-up visits and blood tests (including kidney function and
potassium for some medicines) are part of safe long-term care. Vaccinations and
managing other conditions like blood pressure and diabetes also matter.
